Inside the AI Data Center Boom

By Abdennour T Bada · June 24, 2026 · Last reviewed June 24, 2026 · 7 min read

The defining infrastructure story of 2026 is not a new model. It is the scramble to power the ones we already have. AI's hunger for compute is running headlong into a power grid that was never designed for it, and that collision is reshaping where, and how, AI gets built.

The power crunch is real

The numbers are stark. Goldman Sachs Research projects global data-center power demand will rise about 50% by 2027 and as much as 165% by the end of the decade, driven overwhelmingly by AI.1 By some estimates, data centers could approach 1,050 TWh of electricity in 2026, which, if data centers were a country, would rank them among the largest electricity consumers on Earth.2 The grid, much of it decades old, simply cannot add capacity that fast, pushing operators toward on-site generation and bridging power just to switch new clusters on.

Enter decentralized compute (DePIN)

Scarcity invites alternatives. A class of decentralized physical infrastructure networks (DePIN) has turned idle and independent GPUs into marketplaces that undercut the hyperscalers. In January 2026, leading DePIN networks reportedly generated around $150 million in on-chain revenue from real customers for compute, storage, and data, a step-change from the token-subsidy era.3

The leaders show genuine usage. Akash reported a record ~$5 million in compute spend in Q1 2026, with its AkashML platform serving on the order of 1.7 billion tokens per day of AI inference, offering H100 GPUs at roughly $1.20-1.80/hour versus AWS's $4.50-5.50.3 io.net reported around 139,000 GPUs on its network and pushed toward $20 million in annualized revenue, hitting record utilization in March 2026.4 Across the sector, decentralized GPU networks claim 45-75% cost advantages on inference workloads.4

The pitch isn't ideology. It's a 60-70% discount on inference GPUs, which is compelling even for teams with no interest in decentralization.

Compute and storage, together

Tellingly, the two DePIN categories with the clearest real demand in 2026 are distributed compute and distributed storage, and they reinforce each other. Training and inference generate enormous artifacts: datasets, checkpoints, embeddings, logs. That data has to live somewhere durable, retrievable, and ideally verifiable. As AI compute decentralizes for cost reasons, the data layer underneath it faces the same pressure, which is part of why decentralized storage is having its own moment alongside the GPU networks.

The honest caveats

Decentralized compute is not about to replace the hyperscalers. Its sweet spot is inference and cost-sensitive workloads, not frontier training runs that need tightly-coupled, co-located clusters. Reliability, enterprise SLAs, and support are still maturing, and it is worth separating networks earning real customer revenue from those still propped up by token incentives. The honest read: DePIN is a fast-growing parallel supply of compute and storage, not a wholesale replacement, and that is exactly why it matters during a capacity crunch.

Key takeaways

  • AI is driving a historic power crunch: data-center electricity demand may rise ~50% by 2027 and up to ~165% by 2030 (Goldman Sachs).
  • Decentralized GPU networks (Akash, io.net, Render) now earn real revenue and undercut hyperscalers by roughly 45-75% on inference.
  • Distributed compute and distributed storage are the two DePIN categories with the strongest AI-driven demand, and they reinforce each other.
  • DePIN complements, rather than replaces, centralized data centers, especially for inference.
